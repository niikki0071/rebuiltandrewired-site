Before You Start

If you're holding this, something broke. Maybe you got fired. Maybe you walked away. Maybe life blew up in a way nobody warned you about — divorce, a health crisis, a company that didn't give a damn about you after years of loyalty.

And now you're Googling things at 2 AM like "how to make money fast" and "starting over at 40" and getting buried in conflicting advice from people who've never had to rebuild from the floor.

I've been there. Wrongful termination. Single parent. ADHD brain that was misdiagnosed for decades as anxiety. Bills don't care about your healing timeline.

This Blueprint is not another motivational workbook. There are no vision boards in here. No "just believe in yourself" affirmations. This is a system — six concrete steps to go from stuck to earning, built specifically for women whose brains don't work the way traditional business advice assumes.

Who This Is For:

  • Women rebuilding after job loss, wrongful termination, divorce, or health crisis
  • Women with ADHD or executive function challenges who freeze when overwhelmed
  • Women with existing small businesses who are burning out doing everything manually
  • Women who have skills but can't figure out how to turn them into income
  • Women who are tired of $997 courses that promise passive income and deliver nothing

How to Use This Blueprint:

Work through the six REWIRE steps in order. Each step has teaching content, then a worksheet. Do the worksheets. Not later. Not when you feel ready. Now. Messy is fine. Incomplete is fine. Done beats perfect every single time.

ADHD Brain Hack

If you have ADHD: each step is designed to be completable in one focused session (30–60 minutes). If you stall, skip to the worksheet and fill in what you can. Movement creates clarity. Clarity does not create movement.

Two Tracks Inside This Blueprint:

Track A

Starting From Zero

You lost your income source. You don't have a business. You need to figure out what you can sell and who will pay for it. Start with Step R and go in order.

Track B

Existing Business

You have a business but it's held together with duct tape and adrenaline. You need systems, AI tools, and a way to stop doing everything yourself. Each step has a Track B section for you.

Step 1 of 6

R — RECOGNIZE

Your Unfair Advantage
"What you already know is worth money. You just can't see it yet."

When you lose a job or go through a major life disruption, the first instinct is to start from scratch. New career. New skills. New everything. This is almost always wrong.

You have 10, 20, 30+ years of skills, knowledge, relationships, and hard-won expertise. The problem isn't that you have nothing to offer. The problem is that you're so close to your own experience that you can't see what other people would pay for.

Your unfair advantage is the intersection of three things: what you know how to do well, what you've survived that others haven't, and what people already come to you for (whether or not they pay you).

Professional Skills + Life Experience + What People Already Ask You For = Your Unfair Advantage

Examples of hidden unfair advantages:

  • A nurse who got fired and now helps other nurses negotiate wrongful termination claims
  • A project manager who went through divorce and now creates organizational systems for women starting over
  • A teacher with ADHD who figured out her own productivity system and teaches it to other neurodivergent women
  • A retail store owner who learned AI tools to save 15 hours/week and now teaches other small business owners to do the same
  • A paralegal who spent 20 years helping attorneys and now offers virtual legal admin services independently
Track B

Existing Business Owners

Your version of this step is different: you need to identify where you're doing work that a system or AI tool could handle for you. Step 2 of 6

E — ELIMINATE

The Noise
"Stop consuming. Start deciding."

After a disruption, you enter what I call the Research Spiral. You Google. You watch YouTube videos. You sign up for free webinars. You buy a course. You join a Facebook group. You save 47 Instagram posts. You do everything except make a decision.

This isn't laziness. It's your brain trying to reduce risk by gathering more information. But here's what nobody tells you: more information doesn't create clarity. Decisions create clarity.

This step gives you a Decision Filter — five questions that kill bad ideas fast, so you stop chasing shiny objects and commit to ONE path.

The 5-Question Decision Filter:

  1. Can I get paid for this within 30 days? — If the answer is no, it's a hobby or a long-term play — not your next move when bills are due.
  2. Does this require me to build an audience first? — If yes, it's not your first income stream. Audience-dependent businesses (courses, content, coaching) need existing trust. Start with direct service.
  3. Am I excited about this, or am I excited about the IDEA of this? — There's a difference between wanting to be a consultant and wanting to be someone who says they're a consultant. Be honest.
  4. Can I explain what I'd sell in one sentence? — If you can't, your customer can't understand it either. Complexity is not sophistication — it's confusion.
  5. Does this fit my actual life right now? — Not your ideal life. Your real one. With the kids, the commute, the energy level, the bank account you actually have.
Track B

Existing Business Owners

Your noise isn't business ideas — it's tools and tactics. You've been told you need a better website, a TikTok strategy, a CRM, a booking system, an email funnel, SEO, and 14 other things. Your Decision Filter is simpler: What is the ONE bottleneck that, if fixed, would make you the most money or save the most time? Fix that first. Ignore everything else until it's done.

ADHD Brain Hack

Write down every idea, opportunity, or "I should" that's floating in your head right now. ALL of them. Get them out of your brain and onto paper. Then run each one through the 5-Question Filter. Most of them won't survive. That's the point. You're not losing ideas — you're gaining focus.

Step 3 of 6

W — WIRE

Your First Offer
"One person. One problem. One price."

You don't need a website. You don't need a logo. You don't need business cards, an LLC, a brand color palette, or a social media strategy. You need one person willing to pay you for one thing.

Most people stall here because they try to build the entire business before they've confirmed anyone will pay. This is backwards. Your first offer should be so simple that you could deliver it with a phone call and a Google Doc.

I help [specific person] [solve specific problem] in [timeframe] for [price].

Good examples:

  • "I help first-time home sellers stage their house for photos in one weekend for $300."
  • "I help overwhelmed small business owners set up their bookkeeping in QuickBooks in 2 hours for $150."
  • "I help women going through divorce organize their financial documents in one session for $100."
  • "I help burned-out nurses rewrite their resume for non-clinical jobs in 48 hours for $200."

Bad examples (too vague):

  • "I'm a life coach." — For whom? Solving what?
  • "I help people with social media." — Which people? Which platform? What outcome?
  • "I'm a consultant." — This means nothing without specifics.

Pricing Your First Offer:

Your first offer is NOT about maximizing revenue. It's about getting a yes. Price it low enough that someone says yes without overthinking. You can raise prices after you have testimonials and proof. A $100 sale with a real testimonial is worth more than a $2,000 offer nobody buys.

Track B

Existing Business Owners

Your version of "Wire" isn't creating a new offer — it's packaging what you already do into something clearer and easier to sell. Can you describe your main service in one sentence? Can a stranger understand it in 10 seconds? If not, this step helps you rewire your offer for clarity. You may also discover that AI can help you deliver your service faster — which means higher margins, not lower prices.

ADHD Brain Hack

Perfectionism will try to stop you here. It will say your offer isn't good enough, specific enough, polished enough. Ignore it. Write a bad first draft of your offer sentence. Send it to ONE person today. Not tomorrow. Today. Momentum beats perfection.

Step 4 of 6

I — INSTALL

Systems That Work With Your Brain
"Stop fighting your brain. Build around it."

Most business advice was written for people with strong executive function — the ability to plan, prioritize, start tasks, and follow through without external structure. If you have ADHD or you're operating under extreme stress (which hijacks executive function even in neurotypical brains), this advice fails you.

You don't need more discipline. You need better systems — external structures that do the executive function work your brain won't do on its own.

This step installs a simple operating system for your business and your brain. It has three components: a Brain Dump System, a Daily Priority System, and a Parking Lot.

The Three Components:

1. The Brain Dump System

Your brain generates ideas, worries, and to-do's 24/7. If they don't have a place to go, they circle endlessly and create anxiety. The Brain Dump is a designated place — a notebook, a Google Doc, a note on your phone — where EVERYTHING goes. No organizing. No categorizing. Just capture. You process it later during your Daily Priority session.

2. The Daily 3 Priority System

Every morning (or the night before), you pick THREE things from your Brain Dump. Not ten. Not seven. Three. These are your 1-Now items. Everything else is 2-Next or 3-Later. If you finish your three, you can pick more. But you START with three. This prevents the ADHD overwhelm spiral of staring at a massive list and doing nothing.

3. The Parking Lot

Ideas, opportunities, and "what if I should" thoughts that pop into your head mid-task? They go to the Parking Lot. Not deleted. Not acted on. Parked. They're safe. They're captured. You'll review the Parking Lot once a week. This single system will save you hours of distraction-driven rabbit holes.

Where to Set This Up (Choose One):

  • Paper (The Steno Pad Method): Use a physical notebook. Left page = Brain Dump. Right page = Today's 3. Back of notebook = Parking Lot. Flip to a new spread each day.
  • Phone Notes App: Create three notes: "Brain Dump," "Today's 3," and "Parking Lot." Pin them to the top. That's it.
  • Google Sheets (included with this Blueprint): A pre-formatted spreadsheet with tabs for Brain Dump, Daily 3, Weekly Review, and Parking Lot. Color-coded. No formulas to break.
  • Notion (for the tech-comfortable): If you already use Notion, the logic transfers directly. Create a database with Priority (1-Now, 2-Next, 3-Later) and Status (Backlog, This Week, In Progress, Done, Parked) properties.

Where AI Plugs In:

AI doesn't replace your system. It accelerates what's inside it. Once you have your Daily 3, you can hand tasks to AI:

  • "Write a first draft of my offer description" — AI drafts, you edit
  • "Summarize this article into 3 action steps" — AI does the reading for you
  • "Create 5 subject lines for this email" — AI generates, you pick
  • "Turn my messy notes into a clear outline" — AI organizes your Brain Dump
ADHD Brain Hack

The system only works if it's simple enough to use when you're at your worst — tired, stressed, distracted. If it takes more than 60 seconds to capture a thought or identify your next task, it's too complex. Simplify until it feels almost too easy.

Step 5 of 6

R — REVENUE

Before Scale
"Get paid before you optimize. Proof of concept comes before perfection."

This is the step most people skip. They go from "I have an idea" to "let me build a website and create an Instagram strategy and write a 12-email sequence" — and then wonder why they're not making money six months later.

Your goal for this step is simple: your first $500. Not $10K. Not $5K. Five hundred dollars from real humans who paid you real money for real value. That's your proof of concept. Everything else is theory until money moves.

The 30-Day Revenue Sprint:

Week 1 — Warm Outreach

Tell 10 people what you're offering. Not a sales pitch — a conversation. "Hey, I'm starting to offer [thing] for [people]. Do you know anyone who needs this?" Text, email, DM, phone call. Ten people this week.

Week 2 — First Delivery

Deliver your offer to at least one person. It can be discounted. It can be messy. The goal is completion and feedback, not perfection. Ask: "Would you recommend this? What would make it better?"

Week 3 — Refine and Re-offer

Take the feedback from Week 2. Adjust the offer. Reach out to 10 MORE people — this time with a testimonial or result from your first client. Social proof changes everything.

Week 4 — Stack and Repeat

You now have: a clear offer, at least one testimonial, and a delivery process. Your job is to keep reaching out. The unsexy truth: revenue at this stage comes from consistent outreach, not from funnels or followers.

What You DON'T Need Yet:

  • A website (use DMs, email, or a simple Carrd page)
  • A business name or LLC (you can formalize later)
  • A social media following (you have a phone with contacts — use it)
  • A logo (nobody ever bought a service because the logo was pretty)
  • A booking system ("Does Tuesday at 2 work?" is a booking system)
ADHD Brain Hack

The outreach feels terrifying. That's normal. Here's the trick: batch it. Set a timer for 20 minutes. Send all 10 messages in that window. Don't think between sends. Just go. The anxiety is worst before you start. It drops dramatically after the first three messages.

Step 6 of 6

E — EXPAND

With Intention
"Scale what's proven. Not what's exciting."

Congratulations — if you're here, you have revenue. Someone paid you real money. You have proof that your unfair advantage translates into value. Now the question becomes: what do you build next?

This is where most entrepreneurs go wrong for the second time. They get their first win and immediately try to scale everything — launch a course, build a membership, create a product suite, hire a VA, run ads. And they burn out again.

Expansion follows evidence, not enthusiasm. You only build the next thing when the current thing is working consistently.

The Expansion Decision Tree:

  • IF you have 1–3 clients: Your job is to get to 5. Keep doing outreach. Do NOT build products yet.
  • IF you have 5+ clients and consistent income: Now you can systematize — create templates, write SOPs, use AI to speed up delivery.
  • IF you're maxed on time: Raise prices before adding products. A $200 offer with 10 clients = $2,000. A $400 offer with 10 clients = $4,000. Same work, double revenue.
  • IF clients keep asking for the same additional thing: That's your next offer. Don't guess — let demand tell you.
  • IF you want to build a digital product: Only after you've delivered the service version at least 10 times. Your course/template/guide is built FROM client experience, not from theory.

When to Add Marketing Channels:

  • Pinterest: Best for long-term SEO. Start when you have a product/offer page to link to. Pins have a 3–6 month lifespan (unlike social posts that die in 24 hours).
  • Instagram: Best for building trust and community. Start when you can commit to showing up 3–4x per week. Requires consistency more than quality.
  • Email list: Start collecting emails from Day 1, even if you don't send anything yet. A free resource (checklist, template) in exchange for an email address. This is the only channel you OWN.
  • Paid ads: ONLY after you have a proven offer with a known conversion rate. Never run ads to an unvalidated offer.
ADHD Brain Hack

Expansion is where shiny object syndrome hits hardest. You'll see other people doing things that look more exciting than what you're doing. Remind yourself: they're showing you their highlight reel, not their P&L. Stay in your lane until the evidence says it's time to add a lane.
